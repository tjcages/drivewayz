Leak Detection Procedures. Pressure & Flow.

EMP takes great care in ensuring that all of its products are air tight before shipping to customers. This is through comprehensive pressure tests in water with either pressurized air or nitrogen.

Setting up the Pressure Testing System

1) Double check the pressure booster for the following settings – NEVER ADJUST THIS SETTING; GRAB MANAGEMENT IF SETTINGS DO NOT MATCH.

          - Input pressure (left gauge) = 80-95 psi.
          - Output pressure (right gauge) = 240-270 psi.

2) Adjust the air regulator to 200 psi.

          - Turn the valve below the regulator until the pressure reaches 200 psi.
                    i. NEVER INCREASE PAST 210 PSI.

          - Open and close valve on one of the 10 pressure hoses to equalize air pressure.
          - Readjust regulator to 200 psi if necessary and repeat step b and c until pressure stays at 200 psi.
          - Open one of the 10 pressure hoses and verify gauge on hose reads about 200 psi.

          - Close valve.

3) Add water to tub so that water is approximately 3 inches below the rim.

          - ALWAYS ENSURE PARTS ARE COMPLETELY COVERED IN AT LEAST 2 INCHES OF WATER TO AVOID INJURY IF FITTING DISCONNECTS FROM PART WHEN PRESSURIZED.

4) System is now ready to test Xstream and Rapid-OE.

Attaching and Pressure Testing Parts

1) Standard practices.

          - Never hang part from fitting hose, always lift body of part.
          - Always be careful with parts to avoid dings, scratches, etc.
          - Blow off part completely after pressure testing.
          - Always make sure fittings are completely secured before pressurizing.
          - NEVER PUT YOUR FACE ABOVE THE TANK WHEN ANY HOSES ARE PRESSURIZED.
          - 200 psi is a lot of stored energy. Use caution when testing.

2) Hoses and fitting designations.


3) Attaching fittings.

          - Fittings that key in to part:
                    i. Attach pressure testing fitting completely on the part and then secure locking mechanism.
                    ii. ENSURE FITTING CANNOT COME OFF UNDER PRESSURE – PULL FITTING TO MAKE SURE IT IS LOCKED IN PLACE.
                    iii. Some lock in by twisting lock (2153066-00, 2151816, 2153066-00).
                    iv. One requires screwing down the aluminum lock (2151774-00).
                    v. Correct – Lock fits into fitting, screw is tight, fitting cannot lift off part.



                    vii. Rapid OE and Xstream barbed fittings require a hose and hose clamp. Tighten hose clamp over fittings as tight as possible by hand. Make sure solder joint is visible to look for bubbles.
                    viii. When two barbed fittings are on the same tube use one hose that will connect to the pressure tester, and one hose with a plug


4) After verifying all fittings are secured put part into water, verify pressure on hose reads 0 psi, and then attach hose to coupling

5) Make sure part and fittings are covered in at least two inches of water. Do not stand over part. Open up the pressure valve and verify that gauge reaches 200 psi.

6) If bubbles are being released from the fittings connecting to the part, close the ball valve to release pressure, take out part, re-connect/tighten fittings, and try again.

7) If bubbles are coming out of solder joints or from part put red tag on part and set aside. Notify management immediately.

8) Test part for 10 minutes. Look for bubbles. If no bubbles are present then part passed testing.



9) To disconnect close ball valve, wait until pressure drops to 0 psi, and remove hose from coupling. Disconnect fittings, dry off part, and move part to next operation.

Epoxy Procedures. Mixing & Application.

Epoxies are vital in the manufacturing process of many EMP thermal management parts. They form the connecting layer between the tubing and the plating it is pressed into. The purpose of epoxy is twofold: 1) form a seamless interface for heat to discharge into the tubing and 2) hold the tubing in place. This allows EMP’s products to properly dissipate heat. If there are any gaps in the epoxy, a thermal short will occur and heat will not flow properly to the tubing. This may result in a failure of the system the part aims to protect.

The thickness of the epoxy layer also plays an important role in the heat dissipation performance for a part. Because the epoxy has much less thermal conductivity than the surrounding metal, the layer must be extremely thin to allow heat to easily discharge between the metals. If it is too thick then the heat won’t sufficiently transfer into the tube and it can cause failure to the system. Most customers require a thickness no larger than one thou of an inch.

Parameters of Epoxy:


Different epoxies have different traits that can affect how they are used in industry. For the epoxies used at EMP, it is important to consider the thermal conductivity, viscosity, pot and shelf life, and curing time and conditions.

In general, the term thermal conductivity describes the rate at which heat will transfer through a material. The higher the thermal conductivity of a material, the better the part will perform in removing heat from a system.The difference between an aluminum infused epoxy and a typical epoxy is in the thermal conductivity that the aluminum infused epoxy provides. Although standard epoxy would be sufficient in holding the tubing in place, it would rather insulate the tubing when exposed to high temperatures, thus will generally fail in the system. When we say thermally conducting we mean that the epoxy will allow heat to transfer through it, provided the layer is thin enough.

Viscosity is a measurement of the consistency of the epoxy. The viscosity of the epoxy determines how easily the epoxies can be applied to a part. A low viscosity fluid will flow and spread very easily, whereas a high viscosity fluid will be more difficult to spread. Some of the epoxies that EMP use are easy to manipulate into the tube track to achieve the thin layer, whereas others may be much thicker like putty and be difficult to spread.

Pot life, or working time, of an epoxy is how long an epoxy can be used after it has been mixed. It is important not to use a mixed epoxy after its specified pot life. At this point, the epoxy has cured too much, and attempting to use it can break the bonds and result in poor performance. Additionally, heat accelerates the curing process, and on hot days the epoxy may expire before the pot life has been reached. If the epoxy begins to thicken early, it is important to replace the batch before continuing.

Types of Epoxies:


EMP uses five different epoxies that consist of a combination of resin and hardener. Each of these epoxies offer different properties that can be favorable for different parts.


Mixing and Application:


Mixing the resin and activator is another important step in the process for creating a quality epoxy layer. Refer to the above charts when working with epoxy to know how much resin and activator to mix. The proper ratio is vital in ensuring a chemically strong bond within the polymer.


When mixing the resin and activator we need to ensure that there are no bubbles in the epoxy before application that might cause thermal shorts that could fail the system. Bubbles and air pockets can form if the mixture is repeatedly folded on itself. The best method to avoid air pockets is by thoroughly stirring the epoxy mixture from the center, spiraling outward.

After properly mixed, the epoxy can be fed into a syringe. The tip of the syringe has a ⅛ inch diameter to distribute epoxy as a thin bead into the tube track.


We can use an epoxy spreader to administer a thin layer into the tube track. This ensures a seamless interface between the tube and the plate. The final step is to place the epoxied part into a vacuum chamber to remove all air pockets.






It is important to spread a full layer of epoxy in the track in order to create a seamless interface between the copper and the plate. Protocols for Heat Curing:


In most cases, the epoxy curing process can be dramatically sped up when it is heat cured rather than room temperature cured. Heat curing also allows for a stronger mechanical bond and greater electrical conductivity (similar concept to thermal conductivity but deals with electricity rather than heat). This is often unnecessary in the case of hot and cold plates because the parts are not placed under high stress loads. The benefit to heat curing is in the time it takes to cure compared to room temperature curing.

Note:​ The important thing to note with heat curing is while yes, it may be quicker to bake a part rather than letting it sit 24 hours, we are unable to change any of the manufacturing processes without first consulting the customer of the changes, even if it is an improvement to the process.

Frequently Asked Questions:


Q: How thick is too thick when talking about an epoxy layer?
A: For a thermal management plate to meet customer specifications for performance, the epoxy layer must be on the order of magnitude of one thou of an inch. Having a thin as possible layer allows heat to quickly pass through that layer to the tubing. There is no cutoff point for how thick an epoxy layer can be, but we have seen examples of layers six thou of an inch that have failed.

Q: Once the epoxy is set, can it shrink as it cures and affect the thermal properties?
A: Yes, epoxy can shrink, but generally it is not noticeable. The epoxies we use only see an 0.8% shrinkage during curing which does not impact the epoxy layer between the tubing and plate.

Q: What happens if bubbles are trapped while mixing the epoxy?
A: As the epoxy cures not all of the air bubbles will be forced to the top so many get trapped inside. Once the part gets flycut, the epoxy layer will instead be littered with holes rather than having a flat finish.

Q: If an epoxy starts to get thick and difficult to spread, should I continue working with that epoxy?
A: No, this means that the epoxy has expired its pot life and begun fully curing. Forcing the epoxy will break down the chemical bonds that hold it together, causing a weak hold and cracking. Once you feel that an epoxy is beginning to harden you should mix a fresh batch.


Jitterbug Procedures. Standard & Wet.

Jitterbugging is a type of orbital sanding technique that is used to remove surface impurities and create a homogeneous, cosmetic finish. The jitterbug is a handheld device with a sanding tool on the face that vibrates rapidly in a circle. When an outside force, such as the pressure from a person, is applied downward, the jitterbug removes scratches, dents and burrs created during previous manufacturing processes to reveal a uniform, matte finish. The two main types of jitterbug processes are known as standard and wet.

Coolant Procedures. Measuring & Refilling.

Maintaining proper coolant concentration is important to ensure that machines run properly, and to make sure EMP does not waste money by using too much coolant. Ideally coolant should be between 7% and 8%.




1) Measure your tank with the refractometer. It is in the blue box inside the tool crib window.

           - Using the eyedropper, place a few drops of coolant into the clean refractometer well.
           - Be careful to avoid picking up any tramp oil from the surface of the tank.
           - Wait 30 seconds for the coolant’s temperature to adjust.
           - Close the lid and press go twice to take a measurement.
           - Wipe clean the well of the refractometer after each use.
2) Tell Chris Ells your coolant measurement. He will give you a key for the lock. Unlock the pin and pull it out from the
           left side of the box.
3) Once you have a reading, tell your shift supervisor/ coolant manager the reading in order to get a key to open the coolant dispenser. You will not be allowed to get the key for coolant without a refractometer reading. When you are finished, lock the coolant and return the key.
4) If your coolant is less than 12%/specified cutoff you will be instructed to fill your tank using the red CHARGE button on the coolant mixer. This will provide 7.5% coolant. Put the charge tube into the coolant cow, and hold the red button to fill it with coolant.
5) If your coolant is greater than 12%/specified cutoff, your tank needs dilution. Do not add pure water to the tank. You will be instructed to fill your tank using the green MAKEUP button. This will provide 1.2% coolant. Put the makeup tube into the coolant cow and hold the green button to fill it with coolant.
